Understanding TB

What is Tuberculosis?

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease usually caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis bacteria. TB primarily affects the lungs, but it can impact any part of the body including the kidney, spine, and brain.

Bacterial Infection

TB is caused by bacteria that spread through the air when infected people cough, sneeze, or talk. It spreads similarly to the common cold or flu.

Active vs. Latent

TB can be either active (causing symptoms and spreading to others) or latent (dormant in the body without causing symptoms but could activate later).

Global Health Issue

TB remains one of the world's deadliest infectious diseases, especially in developing countries with high HIV prevalence and limited healthcare access.

TB in India

India has the highest TB burden in the world, accounting for about 26% of the global TB cases. Several factors contribute to this high prevalence:

  • High population density in urban areas
  • Limited healthcare access in rural regions
  • Stigma associated with the disease
  • Increasing drug-resistant TB strains

Symptoms & Causes

Recognizing Tuberculosis

Early detection is crucial for effective treatment. Learn to recognize the warning signs and understand how TB spreads.

Common TB Symptoms

  • Persistent Cough

    A cough that lasts three or more weeks, often with mucus or blood.

  • Fever

    Low-grade fever that may last for weeks, typically worse in the evening.

  • Night Sweats

    Excessive sweating during sleep, often soaking bedclothes.

  • Weight Loss

    Unintentional weight loss and reduced appetite.

  • Fatigue

    Persistent tiredness and weakness that doesn't improve with rest.

How TB Spreads

TB bacteria spread through the air when an infected person coughs, speaks, or sneezes. People nearby may breathe in these bacteria and become infected.

TB is NOT spread by:

  • Shaking hands or touching
  • Sharing food, drinks, or utensils
  • Touching bed linens or toilet seats
  • Kissing

Risk Factors

Close contact with infected persons
Living in crowded conditions
Weakened immune system (HIV/AIDS)
Limited healthcare access

When to See a Doctor

Seek medical attention if you experience:

  • Persistent cough for more than three weeks
  • Coughing up blood
  • Unexplained weight loss with night sweats
  • Persistent fever without clear cause
Diagnosis & Treatment

Fighting Tuberculosis

TB is curable and preventable. Early diagnosis and proper treatment are essential for recovery and preventing spread.

Diagnosis

Skin Test (Mantoux)

A small amount of TB protein is injected under the skin. If a raised bump appears within 48-72 hours, it may indicate TB infection.

Blood Tests

IGRA (Interferon-Gamma Release Assay) tests measure the immune system's reaction to TB bacteria.

Chest X-ray

Can show lung damage that might be caused by TB. Follow-up tests are needed to confirm TB.

Sputum Tests

Laboratory examination of phlegm can confirm active TB and identify the specific strain.

Treatment

Antibiotics Regimen

Treatment typically involves taking multiple antibiotics for 6-9 months. Completion of the full course is crucial.

Directly Observed Therapy (DOT)

Healthcare workers may observe patients taking medications to ensure proper treatment adherence.

Drug-Resistant TB

More complex treatment needed for drug-resistant strains, often requiring longer treatment with stronger medications.

Important Note

Never stop taking TB medication early, even if symptoms improve. Incomplete treatment can lead to drug-resistant TB.

Prevention

BCG Vaccine

Commonly given to children in countries with high TB rates. Provides partial protection against severe forms of TB.

Infection Control

Proper ventilation, isolation of active TB patients, and use of masks can reduce transmission in healthcare settings.

Latent TB Treatment

People with latent TB infection may be treated to prevent the development of active TB disease.

Health Education

Increasing awareness about TB symptoms, transmission, and the importance of seeking early treatment.

Impact of TB on Society

Economic Impact

TB primarily affects people in their most productive years, resulting in significant economic losses for affected families and communities.

Social Stigma

TB patients often face discrimination, isolation, and stigma, which can affect their mental health and delay seeking treatment.

Family Burden

Families bear the financial burden of treatment and loss of income, often pushing affected households into poverty.

Global Action

TB Initiatives & Programs

Global Initiatives

Worldwide efforts to eliminate TB as a public health problem

End TB Strategy

WHO's global strategy aims to reduce TB deaths by 95% and cut new cases by 90% between 2015 and 2035.

Stop TB Partnership

A coalition of 1,500 partners working to increase access to TB treatment and diagnostics worldwide.

Global Fund

Provides financial support for TB, HIV, and malaria programs in developing countries.

Indian Government Programs

National efforts to control TB in India

National TB Elimination Program

India's flagship program to detect, treat, and prevent TB cases across the country.

Nikshay Poshan Yojana

Provides nutritional support of ₹500 per month to TB patients throughout treatment.

Active Case Finding

Screening vulnerable populations to identify TB cases early for prompt treatment.
